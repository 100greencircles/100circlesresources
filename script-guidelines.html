Overview
What This Document Is

The three metrics used to evaluate script quality — AVD, Retention %, and Engagement — measure what the script did to viewers, not what the packaging did. CTR and unique viewers are excluded.

Why these 3 metrics, not CTR
  • AVD (average view duration in seconds) — the moment the script stops being interesting, people swipe. AVD is the script's report card.
  • Retention % — how far into the script people got before leaving. Pinpoints exactly where the problem is.
  • Engagement (likes + comments) — a script that makes someone feel something earns a reaction. Comments mean the script said something that demanded a response.
  • CTR is excluded — it measures the thumbnail and title, not the script.

Section A
How to Write the Script

A 7-step structure derived from what the top scripts consistently did. A skeleton, not a formula.

1
Open with a Hook that Creates Instant Tension
First 3 seconds determine whether someone stays. Your hook must: introduce something unfamiliar (purple cow), signal something changed about something familiar (Nike), or address the viewer directly (Are you a founder who can't get started?).
4 opening formats that worked
  • Named concept: "Here's what [concept] actually means — and why it matters."
  • Curiosity gap: "Most people know [X]. What [brand] did next surprised everyone."
  • Direct address: "Are you a [persona] who [has this specific problem]?"
  • Shocking stat: "[Name], [title] of [company], shared something most brands will ignore. [Stat]."
2
Deliver the Promise in the First 10 Seconds
The hook buys you 10 seconds. In those 10 seconds the viewer must know exactly what they are getting. Do NOT build suspense for more than one sentence. If you use a question as the hook, answer it in the very next sentence.
3
Build the Middle with Concrete Specifics
Vague scripts lose viewers. Specific scripts hold them. The specificity gap is the retention gap.
Use Specific
Named people (Rajeev Jain, Carl Pei, Seth Godin)
Specific numbers (40 team members, ₹2.4–3.6 crores, 7 reasons)
Specific steps (exported from Zoho, pasted into ChatGPT)
Specific comparisons (like iPhone, tougher than the gym)
Avoid Vague
Generic claims ("most companies do this wrong")
Unnamed examples ("a CEO I know...")
Rounded numbers ("a lot of views", "many members")
Generic lessons ("sometimes think differently")
4
Use One Analogy or Contrast Per Video
Purple cow in a field of brown cows. A chat widget that became the iPhone of software. Tougher than going to the gym. One strong analogy does more for retention than three explanatory sentences. More than one analogy dilutes each.
5
Place the Viewer Inside the Story Mid-Script
Any time the script shifts from third-person storytelling to second-person address, retention improves.
  • At least once mid-script, speak directly to the viewer.
  • Use "you", "your company", "your team" — not just "companies" or "founders".
  • Ask a question the viewer can answer for themselves without speaking.
  • Example: "Does your company have a role like this that doesn't have a name yet?"
6
End with a Thought, Not Just a CTA
Day 19: "Everyone can generate content. But not everyone can generate trust." Then the CTA. Day 10: "Why do you think they changed it?" Then the comment prompt. The closing thought gives the viewer something to react to — which is what drives the engagement signal. Never end with just a CTA.
7
Read It Out Loud Before Recording
Every script in the top 11 reads naturally when spoken. Every script in the worst 5 has at least one sentence that is easier to read than to say. Mark every moment you hesitate — those are the sentences that need rewriting.
Section B
Must-Haves

Appear in 3 or more of the top 5 scripts. Their absence from the worst 5 is notable.

1
A Specific Named Person or Brand in the First 10 Seconds
Unnamed examples feel like opinions. Named examples feel like evidence. Day 1: Seth Godin. Day 10: Nike. Day 19: Rajeev Jain, Bajaj Finance. Day 14: Carl Pei.
2
A Number — Specific, Not Rounded
2.7 lakh videos (not "a lot"). 40 team members (not "a large team"). 7 reasons (not "several"). Specific numbers signal research and stop the scroll.
3
One Clear Outcome Stated Upfront
"Here's a LinkedIn hack to turn strangers into warm prospects." "Here's 7 reasons how Intercom became a status symbol." One promise. Fulfilled completely.
4
A Contrast or Comparison That Does the Argument For You
"HubSpot took 6 years. Clay did it in 2." "Do you know the CEO of Xiaomi? Most people don't. But they know Carl Pei." They make the viewer feel the gap without being told about it.
5
A Direct Question to the Viewer Before the CTA
Every question directed at the viewer is a micro-engagement event. It interrupts passive watching and asks for a mental response. That response drives the engagement metric.
6
The CTA Must Be a Single Word or Short Phrase
DM me "Brand". DM me "Influencer". Book a Call. Friction kills CTA conversion. The viewer's thumb is already moving — your CTA must be capturable in 2 seconds.
Section C
Things to Strictly Avoid

Every item below caused viewer drop-off within the first 15 seconds or killed engagement at the end.

01Insider Knowledge as the Hook

Day 17 (Clawd Bolt): assumed the viewer knew what Clawd Bolt was. They didn't. 9.8% retention. The hook must be universally understandable. Niche context can be introduced after the viewer is already watching.

This viral AI project changed names three times. It started as Clawd Bolt.
When a startup gets a trademark warning from Anthropic, what do they do? They change their name. Three times.
02The Well-Known Story With No New Angle

Day 22 (Slack): lowest unique viewers of all 30 (17 people). If you use a well-known brand story, bring a new data point, a current comparison, a personal application, or a counterintuitive reading. The story alone is not enough.

03"Part 1" or "Part 2" Without Full Standalone Value

Viewers do not commit to a series from a cold video. Every video must be a complete unit of value. Structure each part so the viewer gets a complete takeaway from that video alone. Never end with "more in Part 2."

04Borrowed Authority as the Primary Credential

"This is straight from Gary Vee's playbook." The moment you outsource your authority, the viewer has a better option — go watch Gary Vee. Reference known names only to validate points you're already making from your own experience.

05Duplicate Scripts

Days 23 and 29 used the identical script. YouTube suppressed both. Even two videos on the same topic must have a different angle, structure, and examples. Do not reuse any sentence from the marathon version in the sprint.

06The Lesson Before the Story

The story creates emotional investment. The lesson lands harder after the viewer has followed the narrative. Always story first, lesson second.

07Vague Title Promises the Script Doesn't Deliver

"Content goldmine" promised a goldmine, delivered a tip. Either write the script to match a bold title, or tone down the title to match the script you actually wrote.

08Long Warm-Ups Before the Point

Day 3: 8 words before the content started. Worst retention of the top 11 (20.7%). Every word before the hook is a word that is losing viewers. Start with the content.

Here under the tree. Welcome to B2B branding. I recently came across a CEO...
Rajeev Jain, CEO of Bajaj Finance, shared something most brands will ignore.

Sun Script — Viral Shorts Framework
30–40 second
retention loop
A structured framework for writing viral YouTube Shorts. Five phases. One loop. Covers Brand Building, Marketing, AI for Marketing, Growth, and Startups. Negative prompt applied throughout.
Target: 30–40 seconds
Format: Hook → Problem → Insight → Conclusion
Goal: Maximum retention
Overview
What This Framework Is

The Sun Script is a structured prompt-based framework for writing 30–40 second YouTube Shorts. It runs through five phases before a word of script is written — starting with research, ending with visual direction. The negative prompt is built in: every generated script should be checked against the 6 AI writing patterns listed in the Negative Prompt section and stripped of them before recording.

Topic categories this covers
  • Brand Building
  • Marketing
  • AI for Marketing or Growth
  • Startups
Core Structure
The Retention Loop

This is the sequence every Sun Script follows. Every section of the script maps to one of these four beats.

01
Hook
→
02
Problem
→
03
Insight
→
04
Conclusion
  • Hook: Stop the scroll. Create curiosity or reveal a surprising fact before anything else.
  • Problem: Set up the question, tension, or gap the viewer now wants resolved.
  • Insight: The key revelation — the thing they didn't know or didn't expect. This is the core of the video.
  • Conclusion: A strong takeaway, surprising close, or viewer question that drives comments.
The 5 Phases
Research to Visual Direction

Run through all five phases before writing a single word of script. Phase 1 prevents shallow scripts. Phase 5 prevents scripts that can't be visualised.

Phase 01
Research
Before writing anything, quickly analyse the topic and identify three things:
  • The most interesting or surprising fact — not the most obvious one.
  • Why people care about it right now — timeliness, relevance, stakes.
  • What makes it useful, shocking, or controversial — this becomes the hook angle.
The research phase answers one question: what will stop people from scrolling? If you can't answer that before writing, don't write yet.
Phase 02
Hook
First 2–3 sec
Write a scroll-stopping hook. Keep it under 10 words if possible.
  • Create curiosity — the viewer must feel they'll miss something if they swipe.
  • Reveal a surprising fact — something they didn't know, stated in the first sentence.
  • Challenge what people believe — contradict a common assumption about your topic.
Hook quality test

Read the hook to someone who doesn't know your topic. If they don't immediately ask "and then what?" — rewrite it. The hook's only job is to make the next sentence feel necessary.

Phase 03
Main Content
20–25 sec
Explain the topic in simple English. No jargon. Short sentences. High energy.
  • Explain the problem or question the hook raised — give it context.
  • Reveal the key insight — the thing the viewer came to learn.
  • Give a quick example or comparison — one concrete illustration locks in the insight.
The audience should learn something instantly useful. If the viewer walks away unable to explain what they learned in one sentence, the main content failed.
Rules for main content
  • Short sentences — maximum 12 words per sentence.
  • Easy language — if a 16-year-old wouldn't understand it, rewrite it.
  • High energy — write like you're telling a friend something they must know.
  • No technical jargon — if you use a term, explain it in the same sentence.
Phase 04
Ending
Last 5 sec
End with one of three options:
  • A strong takeaway — the lesson distilled into one sentence. "Everyone can generate content. But not everyone can generate trust."
  • A surprising conclusion — a twist or implication the viewer didn't see coming.
  • A viewer question that drives comments — direct and specific. "Why do you think they changed it?" Not "what do you think?"
The ending is also where the CTA goes — after the thought, never instead of it. Keep the CTA to one word or a short phrase.

Style Guidelines
How the Script Should Feel

These aren't about structure — they're about tone. Every Sun Script should pass this feel test before recording.

The script should feel like
Fast paced — no sentence lingers
Engaging — it earns attention every 5 seconds
Conversational — you're telling a friend, not presenting
Natural English — reads exactly as you speak
Avoid
Long explanations — one sentence, one idea
Complicated terms — simplify or cut
Slow storytelling — get to the point in the first 3 seconds
AI-sounding phrasing — see Negative Prompt section
Goal of every Sun Script

Maximum retention in 30–40 seconds. If you can cut a sentence without losing information, cut it. If the viewer's attention could drift anywhere in the script, that sentence is a problem.

Negative Prompt
6 Patterns to Remove From Every Draft

These are applied as a negative prompt during generation. After AI writes a draft, do a manual pass to remove any remaining instances. If you remove these patterns, the remaining text has to carry meaning on its own — which is the point.

01Contrast Framing
A claim is introduced by setting up an opposing alternative as a foil. The structure creates an illusion of insight without adding information.
This isn't about tools. It's about mindset.
02Synthetic Repetition
A point is restated with near-synonyms without adding information. Creates word count, not meaning.
Clarity matters. Precision matters. Intent matters.
03Triadic Closure
Ideas are packaged into groups of three to create a sense of completeness. The grouping does the work that the ideas don't.
People. Process. Technology.
04Declarative Authority
A broad claim is stated with confidence while omitting scope, evidence, or conditions. Sounds certain. Isn't.
AI is reshaping the future of work.
05Performative Vulnerability
A personal admission is used to borrow credibility without contributing relevant detail. The confession does the work the argument doesn't.
I used to think this way. I was wrong.
06Discomfort Signalling
A claim is introduced by announcing it will be hard to hear. The framing carries the weight that the argument does not yet earn.
Here's an uncomfortable truth...
The test

Delete every sentence that does not add a new piece of information. That edit will shorten the script. It will also improve the information density per word. If removing a sentence doesn't change what the viewer learns, the sentence should not be there.
